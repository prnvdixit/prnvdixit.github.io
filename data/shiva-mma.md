His name is Shiva. And his kicks are breathtaking.

That’s it. That’s all I knew about him the morning I walked into the academy and decided I wanted to be *that* good. Two simple lines.

I have been training alongside him for half a year now. And he has won a few medals in state championships and participated in nationals. He has been at it for a couple of years longer than me, so he's my senior - my most terrifying daily appointment. And somehow, that appointment has quietly rewritten how I see every other problem in my life.

The promotion I was chasing? That huge feature release I’ve been bleeding over for last nine months? They are like walking in the sun, uncomfortable for sure but not the end of the world. Because nothing - not a single thing - hits quite like knowing a soft-spoken guy with quiet eyes is waiting to greet you at sharp 7 AM with a storm of punches and kicks. Everything else suddenly shrinks. Everything else feels… manageable.

A few weeks back, the back of my thighs looked like a scenery with shades of blue and green for days. And that was *with* Shiva wearing shin guards. Imagine the canvas without them.

## You become your mind

Martial arts is paradoxical in that sense. It is where the bully and the bullied both end up, levelled by the same mat.

There’s a quiet, almost cruel irony in it: the moment you discover exactly how much damage your hands can do, you also discover exactly why you should never do it. You learn you have nothing left to prove. You learn to walk away from fights — not out of fear, but out of calm, unshakable confidence. And on the rare day life actually tests you, the same art whispers a deeper truth: you were always capable of weathering the storm. You just needed the courage to step into the ring and try.

I started training right after a breakup. And I realised that when I’m sparring, I can finally breathe - even if the rest of my life felt like collapsing around me. Inside that ring there was no room for overthinking. You’re either here — fully here — or you’re on the floor. It contextualizes everything. Your personal problems might be annoying, but they usually don't hurt as much as a 15 year old who has been training since he could walk.

If you choose a good academy, the mat also becomes a sanctuary of meritocracy. It doesn’t matter who you are or what your job title is or what is going on in your life; Show up. Sweat. Grow. That’s the only currency accepted here.

## Storm behind the thunder

Shiva works as a peon in a temporary local job. Master pays his fees at academy because he couldn’t. Looking at him — muscular, compact — you’d never guess he was once a lost cause. Not the “drink quietly at home” kind of alcoholic. The street kind. The kind you’d find sprawled on the pavement at dawn, picking fights with ghosts, chain-smoking his life away.

He begged Master for help. Master moved the entire academy to this neighborhood because of him. I saw the gleam in Master’s eyes yesterday when he told me the story - the kind of quiet pride you only earn when you’ve pulled someone back from the edge.

Shiva isn’t the only redemption story here. Martial arts is full of them. That’s why Bruce Lee, Mohammad Ali, and Abdulmanap stopped being distant legends for me. They became living proof that discipline can rewrite a man’s entire destiny.

## 9 minute dance

Fighting Shiva is simple: you already know you’re going to get hurt. But it’s also strangely beautiful, because there is never a drop of malice in it.
I can land a clean shot that folds most people. He answers with a lightning counter to my solar plexus, then jogs out of the ring to get me water. After one session, I teased him about the map of bruises on my arms. He just smiled gently, pulled up his shirt to show me the matching bruises I’d left on him, and said in his high-pitched, quiet voice: "Sir, lagta toh humein bhi hai, bas face aur dimaag par nahin aane dete." (*Translation: Brother, I feel the pain too; I just don’t let it show up on my face or reach my mind.*)

That is the margin of good and great at anything. Talent and stamina matter, sure. But the one who wins — inside the ring and outside it — is always the one who wants it more.

## Mat is outside the ring too

A great opponent is one of life's greatest blessings. And maybe I've been lucky that I've found one every time I wanted to be good at something.

Martial arts internalizes this in you: We aren't enemies, just two people who made a silent pact to push each other to the absolute limit. If I give him an easy round, I’ve stolen something precious from both of us. The same rule applies at work. My closest colleagues are my opponents too. Nothing pushes the mind like knowing someone is working harder than you. That isn't jealousy; it's nature. It's evolution.

Have you heard of the Empty Boat story? If you are rowing and an empty boat crashes into you, you don't get angry. You just adjust and keep rowing. But if there’s someone in that boat, you scream at him because you perceive this as an intentional act of aggression.

Shiva’s boat is empty. So is the feedback from my teammates when they tear apart a design doc I spent a week perfecting. They aren’t trying to sink me. They’re just the force of nature helping me row straighter.

The day you realize life isn't a zero-sum game is the day you stop fighting shadows.

Every punch is a lesson, and I've taken a lot of punches.
Martial arts taught me that.
Shiva showed me that.
