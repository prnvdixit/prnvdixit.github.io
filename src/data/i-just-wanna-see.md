
## Excuses

You'll always find excuses. Easiest way to beat fear is - "I just wanna see what I can get away with"

For me, it was with
Violin: Long fingers and hands. Left hand dominant
Swimming: Breathing problems since childhood, hospitalized as baby
MMA: Wear glasses
Skating: Way too big feet and pretty tall

Go with the idea - "I just wanna see"

Years ago (when I would sit silently with twitter at breakfast) I read this: You can beat 50 percent of your competition by consistently showing up and working hard.

You can beat the next 40 percent by doing things with urgency and detail. 

The last 10 percent...is simply a dogfight. (https://x.com/PhilBeckner/status/1138456673427894272)

Embarassment is an underexplored emotion, go out there and make a fool of yourself

Was watching ‘Interstellar’ and got reminded of one of the most beautiful (and powerful) concept of science that I use for my own growth path or life choice - ‘Escape Velocity’.
I have a strong belief that every thing, place, person or attachment can keep us tethered only till it reaches a specific point beyond which we just break-off and are propelled free of emotional shackles. That non negotiable point of no-return can take us inwards or outwards but invariably for better.
And only a free & clear heart can muse in hindsight and wonder at missing the signs, red-flags or manipulations that delayed the inevitable escape.
Also, although sometimes it’s good to be as stretchable as a spring but the problem of being a spring is that it does not break, it loses it’s shape & identity when kept on getting pulled for a long time. A spring can’t cut off. Humans can and should.

Start telling yourself "I love it" every time something bad happens about a hobby. Couldn't land that kick? I love it. Couldn't play that note? I love it. Couldn't progress weights? I love it. Couldn't swim a new form? I love it.
Even if you can't find a positive aspect of it, just say "I love it". Train your brain into embracing everything, positive or negative.
All these challenges make the final story better.


I’ve seen this play out very clearly in my career.
I was a junior engineer, and in my first few months I got openly blamed by my manager for “not performing well enough.” Missed expectations, unclear updates, work that technically got done but didn’t inspire confidence. I, like a lot of people in that position, started to write off the manager as unfair, blamed the lack of proper onboarding, or mentally checked out. The manager I had before this one was better you know?

Until I realized that if I let things go their way I am just not testing my own limits of what I can do. And this is not a career advice, but I felt the ceiling of what I can learn in that org was higher than what I would get anywhere in any team or any company in India (and me, the Senior engineers from my then team and even Principal engineers from then team wholeheartedly agree to this even now, that we've done the best work of our career in that team).
So I took it painfully personally. I stopped arguing in my 1:1s. I didn’t defend himself. I went back and asked a harder question: “If this keeps happening, what part of this is actually under my control?”
I realized the problem wasn’t just code quality. It was communication. My manager couldn’t see progress early enough. My design thinking wasn’t explicit. I ssumed silence meant approval. So even if everything that was given to me was getting shipped on time and with great quality, my manager just assumed that it's the easy stuff.
So I changed everything I could control.
I started over-communicating. Daily updates, even when nothing was “done.” I wrote short one pager designs before implementing. I asked for feedback early, sometimes uncomfortably early. I practiced explaining trade-offs instead of just shipping code. I made sure my manager never had to guess what I was doing or why.
Six months later, I was effectively operating at a senior level.
Not because I suddenly became 10x smarter, but because I eliminated ambiguity around my work. Other engineers looped me in their projects. Even though I was 3 years out of college I was leading a cross-org project on my own and leading a group of 8 engineers. I became the person people went to because I reduced uncertainty, not because that I wrote the fanciest code.
It's a different story how all that was still not enough to convince my then manager to see the progress, and I left the company soon after. But all that came in handy when I joined my current company, the ownership and accoutability I showed from the first day was what made me trustable in a new team where I had no rapport. I got an opportunity to lead a multi-quarter, multi-engineer effort and that ultimately led to my promotion to next level too.
And all this surprised me too. What stood out to me was how fast it happened once I internalized responsibility. The blame focused me. I treated feedback, even poorly delivered feedback (full props to me then manager for always speaking in riddles and always giving feedback as a shit-sandwich), as signal.
That giant locus of control is a cheat code. You don’t waste energy arguing about fairness. You spend it upgrading your inputs, your communication, and your decision-making. And over time, people stop blaming you not because they’re nicer, but because there’s nothing left to blame.
